A Lifetime of Work

David had always known that teaching was his true vocation. But after ten years in the profession, he decided it was time for a change of career. He began searching for a new occupation that would challenge and inspire him.

One morning, he spotted a post advertised by a technology company — an opening for a personnel manager. The position required someone experienced in managing teams and finding new talent. David applied immediately. After three rounds of interviews, the employer offered him the role.

As a new employee, David completed a two-week training programme. His director introduced him to every colleague in the department. The executive team made him feel welcome. That same month, the company decided to employ two more specialists to keep up with growing demand.

Within a year, the firm decided to promote David to head of human resources. He was now responsible for the entire employment process — helping the company hire and recruit the right people. He also had to supervise a team of twelve and ensure every staff member felt supported.

Not all news was good. When the business slowed, David had to deliver a layoff notice to twenty workers who had been made redundant. One long-serving manager chose to resign rather than accept a transfer to another city. These were among the hardest moments in his career.

David managed the payroll carefully each month, making sure every employee received their correct salary. The firm offered fair wages and many staff earned a generous end-of-year bonus. David's own income grew steadily, and his annual earnings now exceeded anything he had made as a teacher.

As he approached sixty, David thought about his pension and the benefits the company offered — health insurance, flexible hours, and retirement savings. He planned to retire at sixty-five. Looking back, he was grateful for every challenge and every team he had helped build throughout a rewarding lifetime of work.

The Rise and Fall of Hartwick Industries

The Hartwick corporation was one of the country's most admired enterprises. Founded sixty years ago as a small firm making machine parts, it had grown through careful management into a global business. Its key subsidiary, Hartwick Pacific, operated across Asia and contributed a third of the group's total output.

The company's productivity was exceptional. Its factories could manufacture over ten thousand units a day. Through a series of decisions to incorporate smaller rivals into its structure, Hartwick had built a dominant market position. When it announced plans to merge with its largest competitor, regulators feared it would create a monopoly.

Trade was at the heart of Hartwick's success. The company thrived on international commerce, choosing to export finished goods to markets across three continents while it imported raw materials at competitive prices. This global model had worked brilliantly — until the government changed course.

A new law introduced a steep tariff on imported steel, raising production costs overnight. At the same time, a strict quota limited how much Hartwick could export each year. Supporters of protectionism argued these rules would save local jobs. Critics warned they would disrupt the balance of supply and demand, reduce the confidence of every consumer, and cause the company's annual turnover to fall sharply.

The government offered a subsidy to help cover rising costs, but it was not enough. Hartwick's finance team reviewed the budget with growing alarm. The company had borrowed capital to invest in new technology, and the debt was mounting. Revenue was falling, and for the first time in decades, the firm recorded no profit.

The wider economy made things worse. Inflation pushed up the cost of energy and materials. The national currency weakened, making imports more expensive still. Unemployment rose in the towns that depended on Hartwick. Consumer spending — a key driver of economic growth — slowed to a halt.

Hartwick's board reviewed its savings, now almost gone. Total expenditure had outpaced income for three years in a row. The yield on its investments was far below expectations. Analysts warned that without urgent action, bankruptcy was possible. Even the company's taxation strategy needed reform to reduce the burden on operating costs.

But new leadership refused to give up. They restructured the firm's finances, focused on high-demand markets, and worked to bring revenue back to healthy levels. Within two years, the company had stabilised. The feared recession had tested them to the limit — but Hartwick had survived, and emerged stronger than before.
