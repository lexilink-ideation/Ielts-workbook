Numeration — Practice Workbook

📋 Passage Overview

This passage, titled "Numeration", explores the history of how humans developed the ability to count and use numbers. It traces the journey from early tribal peoples who had only simple number words like one, two, many, through the limitations of medieval European number language, to the key intellectual step of understanding numbers as abstract concepts independent of physical objects.

The passage also examines evidence from specific cultures — including indigenous Tasmanians, South Africans, and the Tsimshian people of British Columbia — to show how early numeration systems worked and how counting ultimately developed into the sophisticated arithmetic we use today.

💡 Reading strategy tip: This passage presents a chronological and comparative argument. As you read, notice how the author uses specific cultural examples to support the main claim that numeration is a remarkable human achievement — not a natural ability. Pay attention to signal words like however, instead, while, although that introduce contrasts and qualifications.

Questions 27–31 ask you to match sentence halves — a sentence completion task. You must choose the correct ending (A–G) for each numbered sentence stem. The options include distractors, so read carefully.

Questions 32–40 are TRUE / FALSE / NOT GIVEN. Remember: FALSE means the passage directly contradicts the statement. NOT GIVEN means no information is provided — don't guess based on your own knowledge.

⚠️ Common trap: For T/F/NG questions, students often mark FALSE when the answer is NOT GIVEN, or TRUE when the passage says something similar but not identical. Always locate the relevant sentence in the passage before deciding.

🔍 Complex Sentence Analysis

Each sentence below is taken or adapted from the passage. Study the structure carefully — understanding how complex sentences are built helps you answer comprehension questions accurately.

"Careful consideration of our system of numeration leads to the conviction that, rather than being a facility that comes naturally to a person, it is one of the great and remarkable achievements of the human race."
Main Clause Careful consideration of our system of numeration leads to the conviction [that …]
Subordinate Clause that, rather than being a facility that comes naturally to a person, it is one of the great and remarkable achievements of the human race
Grammar Note rather than sets up a contrast: the sentence argues numeration is NOT natural — it IS a great human achievement. The phrase is parenthetical (could be removed and the main meaning survives).
Simple Meaning When you think carefully about how we count, you realise that counting is not something humans can do automatically — it is actually one of humanity's greatest achievements.
中文 仔细思考我们的计数体系，会让人确信：计数能力并非人类与生俱来的本领，而是人类最伟大、最了不起的成就之一。
"Our ancestors had little use for actual numbers; instead their considerations would have been more of the kind Is this enough? rather than How many?"
Main Clause Our ancestors had little use for actual numbers
Contrasting Clause instead their considerations would have been more of the kind Is this enough? rather than How many?
Grammar Note would have been = past modal — the author is speculating about ancient behaviour, not stating a confirmed fact. instead signals a contrast with the previous clause.
Simple Meaning Early humans probably didn't need exact numbers. They cared more about whether they had enough of something, not precisely how many.
中文 我们的祖先几乎不需要具体数字；他们的思考方式更可能是"够了吗？"而非"有多少？"
"Perhaps the most fundamental step in developing a sense of number is not the ability to count, but rather to see that a number is really an abstract idea instead of a simple attachment to a group of particular objects."
Main Clause Perhaps the most fundamental step in developing a sense of number is not the ability to count
Corrective Phrase but rather to see that a number is really an abstract idea instead of a simple attachment to a group of particular objects
Grammar Note not … but rather is a classic academic contrast structure. The author corrects an assumption — counting is NOT the key step; abstract thinking IS.
Simple Meaning The most important step in understanding numbers is not learning to count, but realising that the number "4" means the same thing whether you are counting birds, rocks, or anything else.
中文 也许发展数字感最根本的一步不是学会计数，而是认识到数字是一个抽象概念，而非对某组特定物体的附带描述。
"Counting is not directly related to the formation of a number concept because it is possible to count by matching the items being counted against a group of pebbles, grains of corn, or the counter's fingers."
Main Clause Counting is not directly related to the formation of a number concept
Causal Clause because it is possible to count by matching the items being counted against a group of pebbles, grains of corn, or the counter's fingers
Grammar Note because introduces the reason. The argument is: you can count without understanding abstract numbers — just match objects to pebbles/fingers one by one.
Simple Meaning Counting does not require understanding number as an abstract idea, because you can count simply by matching items to pebbles, corn grains, or fingers.
中文 计数与数字概念的形成并不直接相关，因为计数完全可以通过将所数物品与一堆鹅卵石、玉米粒或手指逐一对应来完成。
"It seems that the last is a later development while the first six groups show the relics of an older system."
Main Clause 1 It seems that the last [category] is a later development
Contrasting Clause while the first six groups show the relics of an older system
Grammar Note while here means "whereas" — it introduces a contrast, not a time relationship. It seems that signals hedging — the author is not 100% certain.
Simple Meaning The seventh category (counting with no specific object) appears to be a newer addition, while the first six categories are remnants of an ancient counting system.
中文 最后一类（无特定物品的计数）似乎是后来发展出来的，而前六类则显示出一个更古老体系的遗迹。

🧠 Reading Logic Training

Academic passages use specific logical patterns to build arguments. Recognising these patterns helps you find answers faster and understand what the author is really saying.
📌 Cause & Effect

The passage argues that the development of farming caused a greater need for numeration.

"As they began to settle, grow plants and herd animals, the need for a sophisticated number system became paramount."

Cause: settling and farming → Effect: need for a sophisticated number system. This directly supports Q27's answer (B): A developed system of numbering was necessary when people began farming.

📌 Contrast

The passage contrasts sufficiency thinking with quantity thinking.

"Their considerations would have been more of the kind Is this enough? rather than How many?"

This contrast supports Q32 (TRUE): early tribes cared about sufficiency more than exact numbers. The signal word rather than marks the contrast.

📌 Example / Evidence

The author uses three specific cultural examples to show how early number systems were limited:

  • Tasmania: only counted one, two, many (three terms)
  • South Africa: counted one, two, two and one, two twos, two twos and one…
  • 7th-century Europe: people needed only count to nine to be a legal witness

These examples support the claim that early number vocabularies were often restricted to very small quantities.

📌 Definition / Explanation

The author defines the most important step in developing a number sense:

"Perhaps the most fundamental step … is not the ability to count, but rather to see that a number is really an abstract idea instead of a simple attachment to a group of particular objects."

This definition is the key idea of paragraph 5 and directly supports Q30 (C): thinking about numbers as abstract concepts was necessary for arithmetic.

📌 Author's Attitude / Hedging

The author uses hedging language to show uncertainty about ancient history:

  • "It will never be known how and when this numeration ability developed" — admits uncertainty
  • "It seems that the last is a later development" — hedges with seems
  • "would have been" — past modal showing speculation about ancestors

This hedging is important for T/F/NG: the author is careful not to state things as absolute facts, so some statements become NOT GIVEN.

📌 Chronology / Historical Order

The passage traces the development of numeration in rough historical order:

  1. Earliest tribes: no need for exact numbers (sufficiency)
  2. Primitive counting: one, two, many with gestures
  3. 7th-century Europe: very limited number vocabulary
  4. Abstract number concept: the key intellectual leap
  5. Counting aids (pebbles, fingers) → eventually a memorised word sequence
